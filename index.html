Case Study 01 — Happy Monday

Building a two-sided hiring marketplace from zero — at early stage, with no playbook.

The job board market was broken for small teams. Employers were drowning in unqualified applicants; candidates were ghosted at every stage. We bet we could fix both sides simultaneously.

Co-Founder Sole Designer Product Strategy Brand Growth User Research
Marketing homepage — first impression for job seekers and employers
Situation

The market gap we were betting on

Job boards had commoditized to the point of uselessness. Employers were drowning in unqualified applicants; candidates were ghosted at every stage. The ATS market was dominated by expensive enterprise tools that SMBs couldn't afford or navigate. We saw a gap: a lightweight, beautifully designed hiring platform built for small teams — one that made employers look credible and gave candidates a human experience.

Under the hood, a culture-fit matching layer called SAUCE scored candidates and teams across motivational dimensions — going beyond skills to evaluate what actually makes teams work. But the product's growth challenge was more fundamental: at early stage, design quality had to do the work that paid channels couldn't.

01
Early-stage constraints
Every conversion had to come from product quality and word of mouth — design had to do the work paid channels couldn't yet
02
Dual-sided marketplace
Had to win employers (who pay) and candidates (who give it value) simultaneously — losing either side collapses the product
03
Design as the growth lever
In a sea of utilitarian ATS tools, visual quality and brand weren't nice-to-haves — they were the core acquisition and retention strategy
Task

What I was accountable for

Design the full product experience end-to-end: marketing site, employer onboarding, job posting flow, candidate-facing listings, and the internal candidate management dashboard — while simultaneously shaping product strategy as a co-founder.

Action

The employer credibility insight changed everything

I opened with research, not wireframes. 15+ qualitative interviews with SMB hiring managers revealed a finding that reshaped the entire product direction: employers didn't just want to hire efficiently — they wanted to look credible to candidates. The candidate-facing experience wasn't a secondary concern. It was the primary conversion driver.

01
Discovery — employer credibility as the unlock
15+ qualitative interviews with SMB hiring managers revealed the core insight: employers wanted to look credible to candidates, not just post jobs. That reframed the entire design priority.
02
Candidate-facing job listing — designed to close employers
The job listing page became the product's most strategic surface. Employer branding, salary transparency, one-tap apply. Employers saw the demo and converted because their candidates would see quality.
03
Progressive job-posting wizard
Reduced cognitive load of creating a job post from a long form to a multi-step guided flow — progressive disclosure that got employers to complete and publish faster.
04
Kanban ATS — zero training required
Designed a Kanban-style candidate pipeline that employers could use on day one without documentation. Drag-to-move, source tracking, status labels — intuitive by design.
05
Brand system built in parallel
Design tokens, component library, and marketing templates built alongside the product — so engineering could ship without waiting on specs for every edge case.
Brand

Standing out in a sea of sterile blue

Indeed, LinkedIn, Monster — the entire hiring landscape defaulted to the same safe, employer-first visual language. We made a deliberate choice to go the other direction: bold yellow, candidate-first headline, expressive organic geometry. The brand was a signal to job seekers that this wasn't another faceless job board.

Early brand exploration — candidate-first voice, bold visual identity as market differentiation. This direction didn't make it to production, but it shaped the design language that did.
Prototype

How do you visualize culture fit?

Our culture quiz scored candidates and companies across multiple motivational dimensions — but a flat results screen felt like it was underselling the insight. I wanted something that conveyed multidimensionality: that culture fit isn't a single score, it's a shape. That led to an exploration of the Culture Cube — each face representing a different dimension, rotatable to reveal how a candidate or company mapped across all of them.

The implementation: a pure CSS cube with no JavaScript — state driven entirely by input:checked ~ sibling selectors. Click any face or corner to rotate.

(It didn't ship. Sometimes the most creative solution is the one the PM looks at and says "but why." This was one of those times. The idea lived on as a conversation piece.)

Growth

Structured channel bets at early stage

At early stage, every growth decision had to be deliberate. We ran a full Bullseye channel evaluation — scoring all 19 traction channels on likely impact vs. effort — and narrowed to a focused inner ring to test. The framework pointed to Social/Display Ads as the primary bet.

Bullseye exercise — 19 channels evaluated, scored on impact vs. effort, inner ring identified

I owned the creative execution end-to-end. The campaign led with humor and a candidate-first voice — a deliberate departure from every other hiring platform's visual language. Ads drove qualified traffic. The key learning: top-of-funnel acquisition was solvable. The harder problem was activation — free signups were extremely difficult to convert to paid. That insight would have reshaped the onboarding design if we'd had more runway.

Result

Paying employers. Thesis validated — then COVID.

Happy Monday launched and attracted paying employer customers with no paid acquisition. The employer credibility hypothesis held: employers cited the quality of the candidate-facing pages as the reason they chose us over Indeed. The growth loop was working. Then COVID froze the hiring market. We ran out of runway before it came back — not because the thesis was wrong, but because the market disappeared.

Case Study 02 — Secureframe

Customers were losing deals because they couldn't prove trust. I fixed that.

Secureframe's customers had no standard way to share compliance documentation with prospects. Notion pages, email threads, repeated CSM requests — none of it was working. I led the design of the Trust Center from zero to launch in 8 weeks.

Lead Designer End-to-End PLG Strategy User Research 0→1 Product Design
The Trust Center — a branded, self-serve compliance hub. Compliance status, documents, monitoring, and subprocessors in one shareable link.
Why this is a B2C problem: Secureframe is B2B SaaS — but the Trust Center's design challenge is structurally identical to B2C top-of-funnel. A cold prospect lands on a page and needs to feel enough trust to move forward. Same conversion problem, different domain. Same design tools apply.
Situation

A compliance platform losing deals over a documentation gap

Secureframe automates SOC 2, ISO 27001, and other compliance frameworks. As the customer base matured, a painful pattern emerged: customers were being asked by their own prospects for audit reports, certifications, and policies — and had no standard way to share them.

Some resorted to Notion pages or email threads. None of it met security or branding expectations. Internally, this meant delays, repetitive CSM work, and lost enterprise deals. The product was letting customers down at the exact moment trust mattered most.

01
Customer friction
Prospects asking for compliance docs mid-deal created delays that killed momentum — and Secureframe had no tool to help customers respond professionally
02
Internal support burden
CSMs were fielding repetitive requests to help customers share the same documents over and over — hours of manual effort every week
03
Upmarket ambition
Leadership wanted to move Secureframe into larger enterprise deals — a market segment that would never accept ad-hoc document sharing
Task

What I owned

I led end-to-end design of the Trust Center — a branded, self-serve destination where customers could share audit status, policies, and FAQs with prospects via a simple link. I was accountable from concept through research, prototyping, and launch, working closely with one PM and five engineers, with regular input from CS and sales leadership.

Scope: MVP within 8 weeks. I ran interviews, facilitated ideation, owned all design and UX decisions, and helped shape rollout and positioning.

Action

How I designed it — and the bets I made

I opened with validation, not wireframes. The risk wasn't design execution — it was building the wrong thing. So I started by confirming the problem was real enough to solve fast.

01
Discovery — customer and CSM interviews
Interviewed customers who had lost or nearly lost deals due to compliance sharing friction. Validated with CSMs who were absorbing the support load. The pattern was consistent and costly.
02
Scoping to MVP — ruthless prioritization
Identified the minimum surface to deliver real value: branded page, document upload, real-time framework status, access control (public vs. email gate). Cut tabs, cut customization depth, cut CRM — shipped a focused core.
03
Design — polish as a core feature
The Trust Center's value proposition was credibility. So visual quality wasn't optional — it was the product. Focused on clean layout, clear hierarchy, and an experience that made customers look more professional than their competitors.
04
Testing — simplified through iteration
Early prototypes had tabs. Testing revealed users didn't need them — a single-page layout with clear sections scanned faster and required less explanation. Removed tabs. Added access control toggles. Final flow: ready to share in minutes, no engineering setup.
05
PLG experiment — pre-launch deal seeding
Hypothesized that sales could use in-progress Trust Center previews as a closing tool before GA. Tested with 3 enterprise deals before launch — kept reading below.

PLG Experiment

We tested the hypothesis before we shipped the product

Before GA, I ran a structured pre-launch experiment with the sales team: could an in-progress Trust Center preview close deals? The hypothesis was that prospects cared more about seeing the Trust Center than having it fully built — trust is a signal, not a feature set.

We seeded 3 live enterprise deals with early-access previews. Sales used them in final-stage calls as a differentiator. All 3 closed. Each prospect cited the Trust Center specifically.

Result

Exceeded every target — before and after launch

The Trust Center launched on schedule and outperformed its success metrics across the board. Adoption hit our 3-month target in 2 months. The PLG experiment produced deal influence before GA. And the feature secured additional investment for a second phase.

The Trust Center became a foundational piece of Secureframe's upmarket strategy — not just a feature, but proof that product-led growth could work in compliance software. It secured a second investment round to build CRM integration, usage analytics, and deeper customization.

Why this maps to consumer fintech: Trust is the primary conversion lever in fintech. A consumer deciding whether to link their bank account, share their financial data, or hand over a credit card number is making the same calculation Secureframe's prospects were making. The Trust Center playbook — design credibility as a feature, validate the hypothesis before you ship, measure early — is directly transferable to activation and onboarding flows in financial products.
